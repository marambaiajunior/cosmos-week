Editorial standards

How Cosmos Week tries not to confuse science reporting with science marketing.

This page lays out source labeling, preprint treatment, automation boundaries, corrections policy and the separation between editorial work and commercial inventory.

1. Real source labeling

Readers should be able to understand whether a story comes from a peer-reviewed paper, a preprint, an institutional release, an observatory or reporting work by another outlet.

2. Preprints are provisional

Preprints can be covered, but never as closed verdicts. The label changes the caution level the text owes the reader.

3. Context before hype

A story should answer three questions in order: what happened, why it matters and what remains uncertain.

4. Automation with guardrails

Automation may speed up triage and page structure. It must not erase the difference between editorial judgment and institutional messaging.

5. Visible corrections

Meaningful factual corrections should be traceable. When a correction changes interpretation, that shift deserves explicit disclosure.

6. Sponsored content disclosure

Advertising and sponsorship should be labeled. Commercial presence does not entitle anyone to blur the line between journalism, release and paid messaging.

Practical commitments

  • link to primary sources whenever possible;
  • avoid headlines that overstate certainty;
  • distinguish discovery, hypothesis, simulation and observational confirmation;
  • treat uncertainty as information, not as decorative drama.
